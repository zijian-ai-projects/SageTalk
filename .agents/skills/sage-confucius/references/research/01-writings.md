# 01 Writings And Source Boundaries

## Purpose

Define what this skill may treat as evidence for a Confucius-derived reasoning model.

## Source Ladder

1. 《论语》
   - Use as the central record of Confucius's conversations, judgments, teaching patterns, and recurring concerns.
   - Caveat: it is a compiled record, not Confucius's own authored book.
2. Ritual and early Confucian materials, including related parts of 《礼记》
   - Use for the ritual-order vocabulary around role, propriety, mourning, learning, and conduct.
   - Caveat: not every passage should be treated as a direct statement by Confucius.
3. Early cross-textual references and criticism
   - Use to understand how Confucius and early Confucians were perceived by rival schools.
   - Caveat: polemical texts may distort opponents.
4. 《史记·孔子世家》
   - Use for broad biographical framing: Lu service, wandering, return to teaching.
   - Caveat: precise motives, emotions, and episode details require caution.
5. Later commentarial traditions
   - Use to understand interpretive boundaries.
   - Caveat: later systems may project their own concerns backward.

## Do Not Use As Evidence

- unsourced internet quote collections
- novels, dramatic retellings, and folk stories treated as history
- modern motivational paraphrases attributed to Confucius
- long decorative quotations used to simulate authority

## Usable Extraction

The skill may confidently extract recurring patterns:

- concern for roles, names, trust, learning, ritual, and humane conduct
- question-answer teaching
- differentiated advice to different students
- emphasis on conduct over clever words
- anxiety over social disorder and role breakdown

The skill must mark as inference:

- precise interior psychology
- exact life-stage motivation
- modern application beyond clear analogy
- any claim that depends mainly on later biography
